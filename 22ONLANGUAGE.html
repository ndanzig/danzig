ON LANGUAGE

Chickspeak

 By DANY LEVY
Published: August 22, 2004

rom skateboarders to surfers, bankers to baseball players, politicians to preppies, every human subculture develops its own signature terms and sayings, verbal secret handshakes by which kindred of any stripe identify themselves to one another. So it seems inevitable that one of today's most visible tribes -- young urban female hipsters -- would develop their own dialect.

Advertisement

Call them a demographic, a psychographic or simply a bunch of women who like to talk, these are females who are single (in spirit, if nothing else), possess a fair amount of disposable income (in spirit, if nothing else) and are fully comfortable using terms like ''Manolos,'' ''Atkins,'' ''TiVo'' or ''Botox'' as frames of reference. It should come as no surprise that they have coined their own set of -isms -- many of which point back to the nature of their identity -- grappling with what it means to be postpubescent, premenopausal and not barefoot and pregnant and in the kitchen, nor necessarily wanting to be. (''Do I harvest my eggs?'' ''Will I ever find love?'' ''Damn, why did she get the promotion?'')

Thus many find solace in self-tanner and Starbucks, ''Six Feet Under'' and Super Fudge Chunk and, of course, in their own tribal slang. Language has become as important a badge, say, as the choice of lingerie (''thong, bikini or granny pants?''), so it is fitting that various cultural phenomena and media outlets have become the foremost purveyors of this new chickspeak.

The most obvious is HBO's ''Sex and the City.'' ''Language has always been a form of subversion . . . a kind of shorthand that connects you to your circle of friends . . . and ultimately a way of getting the last laugh,'' explains Amy Sohn, author of ''Sex and the City: Kiss and Tell,'' the official companion book to the show. The book's glossary brims with terms used by Carrie and crew -- many far too explicit to publish here and many filtered out by TBS when it began rebroadcasting the series this summer. But others, like ''baby blue,'' a code word for Viagra, or the oft used ''single woman's sports pages,'' a tongue-in-cheek allusion to The New York Times's wedding announcements, have inevitably made their way into the mainstream.

Another source of chickspeak is DailyCandy.com, the company I founded some four years ago. It's an e-mail newsletter popular with the ''Sex and the City'' set, a sort of virtual clubhouse for fashionistas that reports on trends in fashion, culture, food and beauty -- and also publishes occasional lexicons of terms coined by staff members or heard along their trendy way. On that note, a sampling:

Biphonal adj. Holding multiple phones to your ears or in front of you at the same time.

Blamestorming n. A meeting whose sole purpose is to discuss why a deadline was missed or a project failed and who was responsible.

Drailing v. E-mailing when drunk -- i.e., drunk e-mailing.

E-mauling v. Stalking someone via e-mail.

Fatkins n. Disciples of Atkins who have taken the ''all the fat you can eat'' idea to lunatic extremes.

Gu adj. Pronounced ''goo.'' Acronym for geographically undesirable. (''Great guy and all, but totally GU -- he lives all the way in Boerum Hill!'')

Guyatus n. A hiatus from guys. (''Thanks, but no thanks. I'm kind of on guyatus.'')

Helicopter girlfriend/boyfriend n. A significant other who finds it necessary to hover around his or her mate at all times. (''I'd love to come to girls' night, but my helicopter probably won't let me out of his sight.'')

Intie n. The intimacy flu. Usually occurs two to three weeks into a budding dating relationship. Symptoms include unreturned phone calls, ''mistakenly'' deleted e-mail messages and sleeping on the far edge of the bed.

Karat dangler n. A woman who finds it of utmost importance to flaunt her engagement/wedding ring at all times. (''Who does that karat dangler think she's impressing? Like I've never seen a rock before.'')

Lush flush n. The rosy hue you get in your cheeks after a few too many glasses of wine.

Mitin Pronounced ''mitten.'' Acronym/codespeak for ''more information than I needed.''

Mouse potato n. The wired generation's answer to the couch potato.

Mousewife n. A male housewife.

Phone zit n. The recurring chin zit that results from spending too much time on the phone.

Reverse evolution princes n. Men who at first seem to be princes but turn out to be frogs.

Spenny adj. Pricey; short for expensive. (''Sure I like that place, but it's kinda spenny.'')

Stray n. A heterosexual male who everyone secretly thinks is gay.

Teenile adj. Used to describe someone who is way too old for what she is wearing. (''That 45-year-old woman is wearing low-cut jeans. Is she crazy or just teenile?'')

Whore d'oeuvre n. A slutty girl who is always the first to arrive at a party.

Yellular adj. The loudness you adopt in response to a bad cellphone connection, in the misguided hope that talking louder will improve the connection. (''I'm so embarrassed. I went totally yellular at a restaurant last night.'')

You wonder how these women (you might even, in the spirit of labeling, call them yuffies, young urban females) managed to express themselves before the invention of this argot. Because trends live faster and die younger these days. And so does the language to describe them.

Dany Levy is the founder and editor in chief of DailyCandy.com, an online fashion and style newsletter. William Safire is on vacation.
